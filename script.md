# AI Process Consultant Assessment – Video Walkthrough Script

Hello, and thank you for taking the time to review my assessment.

Rather than approaching this as a documentation exercise, I approached it as if I had been engaged as an AI Process Consultant by the firm. My objective was not simply to document what Michael said during the discovery call, but to understand how the business actually operates, identify the underlying operational challenges, and then recommend improvements that would make the delivery process more scalable, consistent, and AI-ready.

I'll briefly explain my thought process behind each part of the assessment.

---

### Documentation Approach

The discovery transcript was conversational, whereas the deliverable needed to represent how the business actually functions operationally. Instead of documenting the conversation chronologically, I reconstructed it into an end-to-end business process.

I identified the major lifecycle stages beginning with Lead Intake and Qualification, followed by Proposal and Contracting, Engagement Setup, Delivery and Client Management, and finally Billing and Revenue.

I chose this structure because it reflects the natural flow of a consulting engagement rather than the order in which information was discussed during the interview. My intention was to make the process understandable from a business perspective while preserving everything that was learned during discovery.

For each stage, I documented the key stakeholders involved, the systems supporting the activity, the inputs and outputs, important decision points, known exceptions, and areas where additional clarification would still be required.

---

### Assumptions

One principle I followed throughout the assessment was to clearly distinguish facts from assumptions.

The discovery session involved only one stakeholder, the Managing Partner. Whenever a conclusion could not be directly supported by evidence from the transcript, I intentionally documented it as an assumption rather than presenting it as fact.

For example, we know that CRM logging is sometimes skipped for referral clients moving quickly, but we don't know whether that is the only reason CRM entries are missed.

Similarly, we know that project work occasionally begins before formal project setup, but we don't know whether that work is always reconciled afterward.

Rather than making those assumptions myself, I explicitly documented them so they could be validated during future discovery sessions.

---

### Areas Requiring Additional Validation

I also wanted to avoid making recommendations based on incomplete information.

That's why I included a structured gap analysis using Risks, Assumptions, Issues, and Dependencies.

The biggest observation for me was that the entire discovery exercise was based on a single stakeholder's perspective.

Before implementing operational changes, I believe it would be important to validate those findings through conversations with Project Managers, Consultants, and potentially Accounting, together with operational data such as CRM records, project metrics, and billing information.

That additional validation would increase confidence that the recommendations reflect how the business actually operates across the organization rather than from one individual's perspective.

---

### Improvement Recommendations

Once I understood the current process, I deliberately looked for recurring operational patterns rather than isolated issues.

Although the transcript described many individual problems, I noticed that the same underlying themes kept appearing throughout the lifecycle.

These recurring patterns were:

* inconsistent process execution,
* fragmented operational information,
* repetitive manual administrative work,
* and unclear ownership across several stages of the delivery lifecycle.

Rather than proposing separate fixes for every individual issue, I focused on improving these underlying operating principles because I believe solving the root causes produces significantly greater long-term value than optimizing isolated activities.

That's why I organized my recommendations around four strategic themes:

Increasing consistency through standardized process governance,

Improving operational visibility through a single source of truth,

Reducing manual administrative effort through structured automation,

and strengthening communication and accountability through clearer ownership.

Those recommendations are intended to improve the overall operating model rather than simply fixing individual process steps.

---

### Prioritization

I prioritized recommendations based on three considerations.

First was business impact.

I focused on improvements that would positively influence multiple stages of the delivery lifecycle rather than a single activity.

Second was implementation feasibility.

Wherever possible, I recommended improvements that could be achieved using the firm's existing tools instead of introducing entirely new platforms.

Third was dependency.

Certain improvements naturally enable others.

For example, standardizing the delivery process and establishing a single source of truth are foundational improvements that make later automation and AI significantly more reliable.

Without standardized processes and consistent operational data, AI simply automates inconsistency.

---

### Automation and AI Opportunities

For the AI section, I intentionally avoided recommending that the organization replace its existing technology stack.

From the discovery, it was clear that the firm already uses capable platforms including HubSpot, Asana, Google Drive, Slack, email, and an accounting system.

The challenge wasn't the technology itself.

The challenge was the manual coordination required between those systems.

Because of that, I recommended maximizing the value of the firm's existing investments before introducing additional technology.

The proposed future operating model introduces AI as an orchestration layer rather than a replacement for the current systems.

Existing applications continue performing the functions they already perform well, while AI coordinates activities between them using native integrations, APIs, and Model Context Protocol where supported.

I also proposed a number of business-focused AI capabilities including intelligent client intake, proposal development, automated project provisioning, organizational knowledge management, project health monitoring, client communication, billing readiness, and executive decision support.

One concept I found particularly valuable was shifting user interaction away from individual software applications.

Instead of opening HubSpot, Asana, Google Drive, Slack, and other systems separately, users would be able to interact directly with AI using natural language.

For example, creating a HubSpot opportunity could automatically trigger downstream project setup, task creation, folder creation, notifications, and other operational activities.

Similarly, leadership could request project summaries, consultant utilization, portfolio health, or delivery risks simply by asking the AI assistant rather than manually collecting information across multiple systems.

I believe this creates a much more efficient user experience while continuing to leverage the organization's existing technology investments.

---

### Closing Thoughts

If I had to summarize my overall approach in one sentence, it would be this:

I believe AI creates the greatest business value when it strengthens well-defined processes rather than compensating for inconsistent ones.

For that reason, I intentionally structured the assessment by first understanding the current state, then identifying the underlying operational patterns, standardizing the operating model, and only then recommending automation and AI where they could deliver measurable business value.

Throughout the assessment, I tried to balance operational improvement with practical implementation by maximizing the firm's existing technology investments, minimizing unnecessary complexity, and ensuring that every recommendation was directly supported by the findings from the discovery exercise.

Thank you for taking the time to review my submission. I appreciate the opportunity to complete this assessment, and I look forward to discussing my approach further.
